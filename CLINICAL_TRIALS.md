# Clinical Trials — Affordable Prosthetic Solutions

This document tracks clinical trials relevant to our prosthetic outreach initiative, with a focus on affordable, accessible, and low-cost prosthetic solutions.

---

## Primary Trial: 3D-Printed Prosthetic Sockets for Affordable Care

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **NCT ID** | NCT03517774 |
| **Title** | Improving Prosthetic Care for Patients With a Lower Limb Amputation |
| **Sponsor** | Sunnybrook Health Sciences Centre |
| **Status** | COMPLETED |
| **Start Date** | May 1, 2018 |
| **Completion Date** | March 4, 2021 |
| **Enrollment** | 12 participants (actual) |
| **Technology Partner** | Nia Technologies (3DPrintAbility) |

### Key Findings
- **3D-printed prosthetic sockets** performed at the same efficacy level as traditionally manufactured prosthetics
- **Time reduction**: Prosthetic fitting time cut from ~5 days to **under 1.5 days**
- **Cost savings**: Digital manufacturing tool-chain significantly reduces healthcare system costs
- **Developing-world validation**: Prior trials in Cambodia, Tanzania, and Uganda (2016-2017) with pediatric transtibial amputees confirmed efficacy in low-resource settings
- **Quality equivalence**: 3D printed sockets were equivalent in safety, fit, durability, and comfort to standard thermoplastic preparatory sockets

### Significance for Outreach
- Demonstrates a viable, scalable model for affordable prosthetic care
- Eliminates need for plaster casting (reduces material and labor costs)
- Builds on skills of existing prosthetists rather than replacing them
- Enables earlier rehabilitation start, reducing overall length of stay
- Technology is transferable to adult rehab contexts in developed-world clinics

### Links

## Secondary Trial: Myoelectric Implantable Recording Array (MIRA)

| Field | Details |
|---|---|
| **NCT ID** | NCT05768802 |
| **Title** | Evaluation of Myoelectric Implantable Recording Array (MIRA) in Participants With Transradial Amputation |
| **Sponsor** | University of Pittsburgh / NIH (NINDS) |
| **Status** | RECRUITING (started Jan 2026) |
| **Enrollment** | 5 (estimated) |
| **Phase** | Device Feasibility |
| **FDA Regulated** | Yes (unapproved device) |

### Key Findings (Preliminary)
- Implantable myoelectric electrodes enable **simultaneous multi-degree-of-freedom control** of prosthetic limbs
- Eliminates percutaneous connections (reduces infection risk)
- Potentially improves prosthetic control over current surface-electrode systems
- Could reduce prosthesis rejection rates

### Relevance to Outreach
- Advanced prosthetic control could improve quality of life for amputees
- May reduce long-term costs associated with prosthesis rejection and revision surgeries
- Telerehabilitation principles reduce follow-up care costs
- Watch for future cost-reduction as technology matures

### Links
- [ClinicalTrials.gov Record](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT05768802)
- [NINDS Reporter](https://reporter.nih.gov/quickSearch/1U44NS123301-01)
